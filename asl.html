你能教我开车吗?

This is, in fact, my college admission essay

September 15th, 2018

“Video, not Videol,” blurted out my host brother during a family meal. “There is no ‘L’ in it. Your accent must have slipped out!” Although unintentional, his remark left me feeling humiliated. New to this family and culture, I envisioned how others viewed me: just a foreigner with a funny accent.

The next day, I decided to eliminate my accent. I downloaded several episodes of CNN Student News and tried to imitate the intonations and pronunciation of the anchorman. As I tuned my ears to the sounds of the newscast, the actual content of the story became a blur as the words themselves became palpable, all too present. I felt bombarded by subtle English sounds that outwitted my tongue, which was so used to sliding up and down the Chinese language's four slick tones. After countless attempts, I still tripped over certain words. What used to be a manageable language now began to wear me out.

To save myself from more embarrassments, I became sparing with my words. I would hold back my thoughts on hotly argued topics during class discussions. When greeted by my friends in the hallway, I would give them no more than a nod. When the host family members had joyful conversations at the dinner table, I would just sit there and listen quietly. Within a month, I muted my accent by muting myself.

Just as sign language speakers refused to remain silent, I decided to overcome my fears about my accent by resorting to a seemingly silly but gratifying kind of communication: daily self-talking. Standing in front of a mirror and staring at my reflection, I imagined myself as a flight attendant, repeating the safety broadcasts over and over; I imagined myself as President Obama, reciting his 2008 inauguration speech; I imagined myself as Kobe Bryant, delivering his routine pre-game motivational speech to the other players. Imagining my self in these different roles and living out these different lives, I felt the foreign sounds that left my mouth and entered my ears becoming more familiar. My tongue began to retain the memory of the subtle English sounds and challenged the conventional assumption that accents are simply unconquerable.

Now, with newly acquired confidence and an almost indiscernible accent, I started once again to talk with my friends around our lockers before the school day began. As the head of a club, I made student announcements during all-school assemblies with spontaneity and a sense of humor. At a local French market where I volunteered, my detailed explanations of the cuisine often made visitors think I was a native American. Last summer, as a teaching assistant at an English camp in China, I coached an English Speech Contest participant, who felt insecure about his short stature and stuttered through his speech during the rehearsal. Before the contest, I took him in front of a mirror, his head in the reflection barely reaching my shoulder. I smiled and told him, “Yes, you are short, but you'll never be short of words.”
